# Summary: Understanding People

**Source:** [[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987\|Understanding People]] by [[Larry Crabb\|Larry Crabb]] (1987)

**See also:** [[resources/notes/2026-04-13 Summary - Organize for Complexity\|Summary: Organize for Complexity (Pflaeging)]] for a companion summary and [[resources/notes/2026-04-13 Synthesis - Pflaeging and Crabb\|cross-book synthesis]].

## The Core Argument

Crabb wants to build a model of biblical counseling that takes people's interior lives as seriously as their behavior. His premise: personal problems (those not caused by organic malfunction) trace back to a broken relationship with God and a commitment to something higher than knowing God ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 210\|p. 210]]). The Bible provides sufficient categories of understanding to address every relational issue of life, though it does not answer every question directly ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 106\|pp. 105–106]]).

Three assumptions govern his framework ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 32\|pp. 32–33]]):

1. The Bible provides a sufficient framework for every question a counselor needs to ask.
2. Relationship with Christ provides resources indispensable in resolving every non-organic psychological problem.
3. The community of God's people functioning in biblical relationship is the intended context for understanding and living out God's answers.

## Fallen Image-Bearers

Crabb's starting point for understanding people: we are fallen image-bearers ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 146\|p. 146]]). We resemble God (the image) and we are broken (the Fall). Genesis 1–2 records the image; [[resources/bible/pentateuch/Genesis 3\|Genesis 3]] records the fall. From [[resources/bible/pentateuch/Genesis 4\|Genesis 4]] onward, Scripture treats humans as beings marked by both dignity and depravity ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 146\|p. 146]]).

The image of God shows up in four capacities shared by God and people ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 163\|pp. 160–163]]):

1. **Deep Longings.** God laments wayward children with the intensity of his whole being ([[resources/bible/prophets/Hosea 11#11:8\|Hosea 11#11:8]]). The psalmist pants for God the way a deer pants for water ([[resources/bible/writings/Psalm 42#42:1\|Psalm 42#42:1]]). Both God and humans long for satisfaction at the deepest level of personality.
2. **Evaluative Thinking.** God observed humanity's wickedness and formed conclusions ([[resources/bible/pentateuch/Genesis 6#6:5\|Genesis 6#6:5]]). Humans observe, form impressions, organize them into beliefs, and direct their lives accordingly.
3. **Active Choosing.** God purposes and acts according to the counsel of his will ([[resources/bible/epistles/Ephesians 1#1:9\|Ephesians 1#1:9]]–11). Humans set directions and pursue them ([[resources/bible/epistles/Philippians 2#2:12\|Philippians 2#2:12]]–13).
4. **Emotional Experiencing.** Jesus wept at Lazarus' death and felt anger at the temple's commercialization. Nehemiah wept, Job churned inside, Paul felt perplexed. Both God and humans feel as they interact with the world.

The critical difference: God is independent. Humans are dependent. We need outside help for both physical and personal existence. The essence of sin is a refusal to admit this dependence, "an arrogant and foolish claim to an independence that simply is not there" ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 164\|p. 164]]).

## The Hollow Core and Three Levels of Longing

Crabb introduces the "Hollow Core" ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 180\|p. 180]]): the deepest center of personality that, when full, produces wholeness and joy, and when empty, produces unbearable ache and loneliness. Christ offers a Full Core.

He maps three concentric circles of longing ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 203\|pp. 203–205]]):

- **Casual longings:** desires for convenience, comfort, and personal preference.
- **Critical longings:** legitimate hopes for deep human relationships and visible impact.
- **Crucial longings:** the deep thirsts of our inmost being, which only Christ can satisfy.

God created us for relationship and for impact ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 190\|p. 190]]). We long to be part of an eternal plan and to make a lasting difference. These longings are legitimate. The problem begins when fallen image-bearers turn away from God to satisfy them independently. Selfishness has its roots not in the longings themselves, but in an arrogant determination to pursue satisfaction apart from God ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 186\|p. 186]]; cf. [[resources/zotero/piperDesiringGodMeditations2011\|piperDesiringGodMeditations2011]], [[resources/zotero/smithDesiringKingdomWorship2009\|smithDesiringKingdomWorship2009]]).

Christian joy is a by-product of following Christ, not an end pursued on our own terms ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 200\|p. 200]]).

## The Relational Model vs. Dynamic and Moral Models

Crabb positions his framework against two alternatives ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 138\|p. 138]]):

The **Dynamic Model** sees emotional distress as caused by deep psychological processes that must be exposed. The **Moral Model** focuses on irresponsible choices and behavioral patterns, with little attention to motives beneath behavior ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 138\|p. 138]]; cf. [[resources/bible/prophets/Jeremiah 17#17:9\|Jeremiah 17#17:9]], [[resources/bible/writings/Proverbs 19#19:21\|Proverbs 19#19:21]], [[resources/bible/epistles/Hebrews 4#4:12\|Hebrews 4#4:12]]).

Crabb's **Relational Model** holds that the most significant fact about people is that they were made to love and be loved ([[resources/bible/pentateuch/Genesis 1#1:27\|Genesis 1#1:27]]–28, [[resources/bible/pentateuch/Genesis 2#2:18\|Genesis 2#2:18]], [[resources/bible/writings/Ecclesiastes 4#4:9\|Ecclesiastes 4#4:9]]). Human problems are defensive attempts to handle the pain of fear and tension in significant relationships. People get caught in a vicious cycle: hurt, defensive retreat, more hurt, more retreat ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 139\|p. 139]]).

## Sin as Independence

Sin, in Crabb's framework, extends far beyond individual misbehavior. It resembles a psychological complex: "an organic network of compulsive attitudes, beliefs, and behavior deeply rooted in an alienation from God" ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 221\|pp. 221–222]]). Sinful thoughts, words, and deeds flow from a darkened heart automatically and compulsively, like water from a polluted fountain.

The Reformers saw that fallen human nature was touched in every area by original sin ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 219\|p. 219]]). Without the Spirit's movement, people have a natural distaste for God, an uncontrollable desire to break his laws, and a constant tendency to sit in judgment on him ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 220\|p. 220]]).

Adam rejected the path of dependence, believing a superior brand of life was available through separateness from God ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 227\|p. 227]]). That rebellion stained every subsequent thought with a lie: "a better life can be found through independent self-expression and self-determination" ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 228\|p. 228]]).

## The Waterline: Conscious and Unconscious Dynamics

Crabb uses an iceberg metaphor ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 251\|pp. 251–252]]). Above the waterline: exegesis, proclamation, exhortation to obedience, encouragement to persevere. These are vital. But if no work happens below the waterline, work above it produces externalism: visible conformity to local standards with internal emptiness and corruption that blocks enjoyment of God and involvement with others.

Pastors who work only above the waterline produce either robots or rebels ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 252\|p. 252]]). The congregation fills with Pharisees, clean above the waterline but corrupt below it.

Two elements operate below the waterline ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 257\|p. 257]]): **denied relational pain** (arising from disappointed longings) and **self-protective patterns of relating**. Our strategies maintain a balance: close enough to others to be affirmed, far enough away to run little risk of serious hurt ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 260\|p. 260]]). See [[resources/zotero/hendricksOtherHalfChurch2020\|The other half of church]] for what happens when we operate like this.

**People select painful self-images (inept, stupid, ugly) because even those are less threatening than acknowledged helplessness. The image gives them something to fix, something to control ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 239\|p. 239]]).**

## Change: Repentance, Dependence, and Restored Choice

Real change means change in the inner person, where a deceitful heart and a darkened mind must be exposed and confronted by God's message ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 225\|p. 225]]). External improvement without internal renewal is hypocrisy ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 233\|p. 233]]).

Crabb's equation: Biblical Categories × Life's Observations × Reflection = Biblical Understanding ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 121\|p. 121]]). This process repeats. The message you teach penetrates others no more than it has penetrated you ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 122\|p. 122]]).

**God provides three instruments for self-exposure ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 255\|p. 255]]): the Word of God ([[resources/bible/epistles/Hebrews 4#4:12\|Hebrews 4#4:12]]–13), the Spirit of God ([[resources/bible/writings/Psalm 139#139:23\|Psalm 139#139:23]]–24), and the People of God ([[resources/bible/epistles/Hebrews 3#3:13\|Hebrews 3#3:13]]).**

The volitional capacity has two parts: the capacity to choose behavior (V-1) and the capacity to choose goals (V-2) ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 280\|p. 280]]). Behavior feels like a choice to the degree that the goal behind it is recognized. Behavior in pursuit of an unrecognized goal feels compulsive ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 290\|p. 290]]). Restoring the feeling of choice requires exposing fundamental goals, especially the self-protective purposes that corrupt our directions ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 296\|p. 296]]).

Repentance has two movements ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 264\|p. 264]]): (1) identify interactions where protection from personal pain has become a higher priority than obedience, and (2) replace self-protective manipulation with vulnerable obedience. Then forgiveness of those who hurt you and movement toward involvement, not retreat ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 265\|pp. 265–267]]). See also [[resources/zotero/winshipLivingFearlessExchanging2022\|Living Fearless]] by Winship.

## A Portrait of Health

Healthy people enjoy God with occasional bursts of ecstasy followed by long periods of quiet allegiance ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 215\|pp. 215–216]]). They move toward others, not away. Involvement, not retreat, is their lifestyle. They experience a "marred joy," a life lived in the minor key with eager anticipation of the day the Master Musician strikes up the eternal anthem in the major key. They welcome confusion because it deepens their vulnerability to being led by someone who is not confused. They define faith as the courage to move on in the absence of clarity.

Life becomes "discouraging at times, often wearisome, and occasionally very painful, but in a slowly growing way, quietly thrilling" ([[resources/zotero/crabbUnderstandingPeopleDeep1987#Page 298\|p. 298]]).
