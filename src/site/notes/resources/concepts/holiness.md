Being set apart for God's purposes. The Hebrew *qadosh* means separate, distinct, other. God commands his people to be holy because he is holy ([[resources/bible/pentateuch/Leviticus 11#11:44\|Leviticus 11#11:44]], [[resources/bible/pentateuch/Leviticus 19#19:2\|Leviticus 19#19:2]], [[resources/bible/epistles/1 Peter 1#1:15-16\|1 Peter 1#1:15-16]]). This is both a status (believers are sanctified in Christ, [[resources/bible/epistles/1 Corinthians 1#1:2\|1 Corinthians 1#1:2]], [[resources/bible/epistles/Hebrews 10#10:10\|Hebrews 10#10:10]]) and a process (believers grow into that identity, [[resources/bible/epistles/Philippians 2#2:12-13\|Philippians 2#2:12-13]]).

Kevin DeYoung distinguishes these two senses: "sanctified is what we are and what we must become." The positional reality drives the progressive pursuit, not the other way around.

In the OT, holiness was contagious in a dangerous way: priestly garments could transmit it to unprepared people ([[resources/bible/prophets/Ezekiel 44#44:19\|Ezekiel 44#44:19]]). Jesus reversed this. Instead of becoming unclean by touching the unclean, he transmitted his cleanness to them ([[resources/bible/gospels/Luke 8#8:44\|Luke 8#8:44]], [[resources/bible/gospels/Mark 1#1:41\|Mark 1#1:41]]). The direction of holiness changed at the incarnation.

Holiness connects to [[resources/concepts/obedience\|obedience]] (Peter ties them together in [[resources/bible/epistles/1 Peter 1#1:14-16\|1 Peter 1#1:14-16]]) and sits in tension with legalism. Ferguson shows that both legalism and antinomianism misread the relationship between law and gospel. The antidote to both is Christ himself.
