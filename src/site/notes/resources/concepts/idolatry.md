Giving to any created thing the worship, trust, or devotion that belongs to God alone. Scripture treats idolatry as spiritual adultery: Israel is a bride who abandons her husband for lovers ([[resources/bible/prophets/Ezekiel 16\|Ezekiel 16]], [[resources/bible/prophets/Hosea 2#2:2-13\|Hosea 2#2:2-13]]). The prophets mock the absurdity of bowing to what your own hands carved ([[resources/bible/prophets/Isaiah 44#44:9-20\|Isaiah 44#44:9-20]]).

G.K. Beale's central insight: worshipers become like what they worship ([[resources/bible/writings/Psalm 115#115:8\|Psalm 115#115:8]], [[resources/bible/writings/Psalm 135#135:18\|Psalm 135#135:18]]). Idolatry does not leave the worshiper unchanged. Those who worship deaf, blind, mute objects grow deaf, blind, and mute to God. The same principle works in reverse: those who behold God's [[resources/concepts/glory of God\|glory]] are transformed into his image ([[resources/bible/epistles/2 Corinthians 3#3:18\|2 Corinthians 3#3:18]]).

Ezekiel names Israel's idolatry in escalating terms: unfaithfulness ([[resources/bible/prophets/Ezekiel 16\|Ezekiel 16]]), prostitution ([[resources/bible/prophets/Ezekiel 23\|Ezekiel 23]]), and child sacrifice ([[resources/bible/prophets/Ezekiel 23#23:37-39\|Ezekiel 23#23:37-39]]). The consequence: God withdraws his presence from the [[resources/concepts/temple\|temple]].

Modern idolatry takes subtler forms. [[Timothy Keller\|Timothy Keller]] defined an idol as anything so central to your life that losing it would make life feel meaningless. Richard Lints traces how the [[resources/concepts/image of God\|image of God]] in humanity gets inverted when we worship images we create.
