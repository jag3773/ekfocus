The human response God requires to his revealed will. Obedience in Scripture is not mechanical compliance with rules. It flows from relationship: "If you love me, you will keep my commandments" ([[resources/bible/gospels/John 14#14:15\|John 14#14:15]]). Ezekiel locates life itself in following God's commands ([[resources/bible/prophets/Ezekiel 20#20:11\|Ezekiel 20#20:11]], [[resources/bible/pentateuch/Deuteronomy 4#4:8\|Deuteronomy 4#4:8]]).

In middle school I realized that what God is after in us is simply **willingness**.  The willingness to obey–the willingness to do what he asks.

Peter weaves obedience through his first letter three times in chapter one alone ([[resources/bible/epistles/1 Peter 1#1:2\|1 Peter 1#1:2]], [[resources/bible/epistles/1 Peter 1#1:14\|1 Peter 1#1:14]], [[resources/bible/epistles/1 Peter 1#1:22\|1 Peter 1#1:22]]), connecting it to purification and the capacity to love. Obedience purifies the soul; a purified soul can love other people. The sequence matters.

Paul frames the same truth through freedom: Christ freed believers from the law's curse ([[resources/bible/epistles/Galatians 5#5:1\|Galatians 5#5:1]]) so they could serve one another through love ([[resources/bible/epistles/Galatians 5#5:13\|Galatians 5#5:13]]). Peter echoes this: freedom in Christ is for service, not a cover for sin ([[resources/bible/epistles/1 Peter 2#2:16\|1 Peter 2#2:16]]). James closes the loop: faith without obedient action is dead ([[resources/bible/epistles/James 2#2:17\|James 2#2:17]]).

Obedience sits between [[resources/concepts/justification by faith\|justification by faith]] (which grounds our standing before God) and [[resources/concepts/holiness\|holiness]] (which describes the life that results). It connects to [[resources/concepts/humility\|humility]], since obedience requires submitting your will to another's.

## DMM/CPM and Obedience

Disciple-making movements treat obedience as the engine of multiplication. [[resources/concepts/Discovery Bible Study\|Discovery Bible Study]] asks participants "what will you obey from this passage?" before the meeting ends, making obedience the immediate response to encounter with Scripture rather than a downstream aspiration. Trousdale calls this "obedience-based discipleship" and contrasts it with knowledge-based models that accumulate information without expecting action.

The first-century patterns share this instinct: new believers obeyed and taught others to obey before they had formal training ([[resources/notes/2022-08-11 Patterns Worth Repeating - First Century Practices\|2022-08-11 Patterns Worth Repeating - First Century Practices]]). Paul expected the same from the Thessalonians, telling them to hold to the traditions they received ([[resources/notes/2021-02-20 Follow Apostolic Traditions Written and Oral\|2021-02-20 Follow Apostolic Traditions Written and Oral]]).
