The theory and method of interpretation, especially of biblical texts. Where [[resources/concepts/exegesis\|exegesis]] asks "what does this text mean?", hermeneutics asks "how do we determine what texts mean?" The discipline addresses questions of authorial intent, genre, historical context, literary form, the reader's own location, and how all of these interact when someone opens Scripture.

## Why It Matters

The note on [[resources/notes/2018-Seminary Purpose\|2018-Seminary Purpose]] captures the conviction that shaped much of my thinking: the purpose of seminary—and really of any theological formation—is to teach people how to interpret the Bible, not merely to transfer theological conclusions. Hermeneutics is the discipline that makes that possible. Without it, interpretation defaults to [[resources/concepts/eisegesis\|eisegesis]], importing the reader's assumptions onto the text rather than drawing meaning out of it.

This is also the foundation of [[resources/concepts/Bible translation\|Bible translation]]. Every translation is already an act of interpretation ([[resources/notes/Sum-2020-12-06-How to Read the Bible for All Its Worth\|Fee and Stuart's point]]), which means translators need hermeneutical competence, not just linguistic skill. The [[resources/notes/2025-03-25 Kite Mind Map of Factors for Effective CBBT\|2025-03-25 Kite Mind Map of Factors for Effective CBBT]] places theological formation—rooted in sound hermeneutics—as a prerequisite for church-based translation work.

## Core Tensions

Three tensions run through the hermeneutics resources in this vault:

**Text vs. Reader.** [[resources/zotero/osborneHermeneuticalSpiralComprehensive2006\|Osborne's hermeneutical spiral]] describes interpretation as a movement between the horizon of the text and the horizon of the reader, converging toward intended meaning. [[resources/zotero/vanhoozerThereMeaningThis2009\|Vanhoozer]] argues more forcefully that texts carry determinate meaning rooted in authorial intention and that readers have a moral responsibility to receive it. [[resources/zotero/thiseltonHermeneuticsIntroduction2009\|Thiselton]] traces the history of this debate from Schleiermacher through Gadamer and Ricoeur.

**Print vs. Oral.** Western hermeneutics has been shaped by print culture—fixed texts, grammatical analysis, propositional extraction. [[resources/zotero/steffenReturnOralHermeneutics2020\|Steffen, Bjoraker, and Shaw]] argue this created a blind spot: the Bible was composed, transmitted, and first interpreted in oral-aural cultures. Their work raises the question of whether methods calibrated for literate, individualistic reading are sufficient for the majority of the global church who are oral-preference learners. See also [[resources/concepts/MOC The Importance of Storying\|MOC The Importance of Storying]] and [[resources/concepts/Discovery Bible Study\|Discovery Bible Study]].

**Method vs. Meaning.** [[resources/zotero/feeHowReadBible2003\|Fee and Stuart]] and the [[resources/notes/2012-09 12 Steps of Exegesis - Fee Stuart\|12 Steps of Exegesis]] provide a procedural method—step-by-step exegetical process scaled for pastors. [[resources/zotero/rykenWordsDelightLiterary1992\|Ryken]] insists that method must attend to literary form, because the Bible's meaning is inseparable from how it communicates. [[resources/zotero/vanhoozerMereChristianHermeneutics2024\|Vanhoozer's Mere Christian Hermeneutics]] tries to hold these together under a theological framework centered on the Transfiguration: faithful figural reading is not a departure from the literal sense but its glorification.

## The Problem of [[resources/concepts/Vertical Transference\|Vertical Transference]]

One of the most practical hermeneutical concepts in this vault. [[resources/concepts/Vertical Transference\|Vertical Transference]] describes how readers import their own perspective onto the text or context of another. The remedy is to first understand the context of the original writer and hearers before asking what the text means for us today. This is the move that [[resources/concepts/exegesis\|exegesis]] makes, and the reason [[resources/zotero/feeNewTestamentExegesis2002\|Fee]] and [[resources/zotero/stuartOldTestamentExegesis2009\|Stuart]] begin with historical-grammatical work before moving to application.

## Hermeneutics and Translation

Hermeneutics intersects with Bible translation at several points throughout this vault:

- [[resources/notes/2014-06 Translation Paradigms\|2014-06 Translation Paradigms]] tracks how translation methodology has shifted from expat-led, linguistics-centric work to church-led, exegesis-centric approaches
- [[resources/notes/2024-11-21 Evaluating Bible Translation - ETEN Innovation Lab\|2024-11-21 Evaluating Bible Translation - ETEN Innovation Lab]] argues for multimodal evaluation frameworks that recognize embodied and oral dimensions of meaning-making
- [[resources/notes/Four Qualities of a Good Translation - TUAA and CANA\|Four Qualities of a Good Translation - TUAA and CANA]] defines quality criteria that depend on hermeneutical competence in the translating community
- [[resources/notes/2019-10 Pragmatic appeal for a holistic approach to BT\|2019-10 Pragmatic appeal for a holistic approach to BT]] envisions translation teams with specialized exegetical expertise alongside pastoral and technical skills

## Historical Roots

The history of hermeneutics as a discipline is long. [[resources/zotero/augustineChristianDoctrine\|Augustine's *On Christian Doctrine*]] (397/426 AD) is the earliest systematic treatment of how to interpret Scripture, addressing signs, things, and the role of charity as interpretive guide. [[resources/zotero/thiseltonHermeneuticsIntroduction2009\|Thiselton]] surveys the modern development from Schleiermacher's focus on understanding the author, through Dilthey's distinction between explanation and understanding, Gadamer's fusion of horizons, Ricoeur's narrative theory, and postmodern critiques—including [[resources/notes/2025-02-02 Thiselton and Lyotard on Information, Power, Computers\|Lyotard's challenge to metanarratives]], which raises questions about how knowledge, power, and technology reshape interpretation today.
