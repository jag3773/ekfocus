The visible, weighty presence of God among his people. The Hebrew *kavod* carries the sense of heaviness or significance. In Exodus, God's glory filled the [[resources/concepts/tabernacle\|tabernacle]] ([[resources/bible/pentateuch/Exodus 40#40:34\|Exodus 40#40:34]]). Solomon saw it fill the [[resources/concepts/temple\|temple]] ([[resources/bible/prophets/1 Kings 8#8:10-11\|1 Kings 8#8:10-11]]). Ezekiel watched it leave ([[resources/bible/prophets/Ezekiel 10#10:18-19\|Ezekiel 10#10:18-19]]) and later return to a restored temple ([[resources/bible/prophets/Ezekiel 43#43:1-5\|Ezekiel 43#43:1-5]]). The New Testament transfers this presence to Jesus ([[resources/bible/gospels/John 1#1:14\|John 1#1:14]]) and to the church as God's dwelling ([[resources/bible/epistles/2 Corinthians 3#3:18\|2 Corinthians 3#3:18]], [[resources/bible/epistles/Ephesians 2#2:21-22\|Ephesians 2#2:21-22]]).

The departure-and-return arc in Ezekiel is one of Scripture's most striking narrative threads. God's glory leaves because of Israel's [[resources/concepts/idolatry\|idolatry]], but God promises to dwell again among a people given a [[new heart\|new heart]]. Revelation closes the story: the New Jerusalem needs no temple because God himself is its light ([[resources/bible/epistles/Revelation 21#21:22-23\|Revelation 21#21:22-23]]).
