## Summary

Archippus appears in two New Testament verses. Both show a man entrusted with ministry responsibility in the Colossian church.

In [[resources/bible/epistles/Philemon 1#1:2\|Philemon 1#1:2]], Paul addresses his letter to "Philemon our beloved fellow worker, [[resources/people/pauline team/Apphia\|Apphia]] our sister, Archippus our fellow soldier (*systratiōtēs*), and the church in your house." Paul uses "fellow soldier" for only Archippus and [[resources/people/pauline team/Epaphroditus\|Epaphroditus]] ([[resources/bible/epistles/Philippians 2#2:25\|Philippians 2#2:25]]). The term carries military overtones: ministry as spiritual combat. Archippus had endured hardship or conflict alongside Paul. His naming with [[resources/people/pauline team/Philemon\|Philemon]] and [[resources/people/pauline team/Apphia\|Apphia]] in a personal letter suggests he belonged to Philemon's household, probably as his son, though the text does not specify.

In [[resources/bible/epistles/Colossians 4#4:17\|Colossians 4#4:17]], Paul routes a public message through the Colossian congregation: "Say to Archippus, 'See to it that you complete the ministry (*diakonia*) you have received in the Lord.'" The word *diakonia* points to a particular assignment but no other details are provided. Given the warm "fellow soldier" in Philemon, this seems to be public encouragement rather than a critique. Archippus may have been a local shepherd among the the Colossian churches. 

Archippus represents the next generation of leaders in the Pauline churches: younger equipping servants entrusted with *diakonia* who needed encouragement and exhortation.
