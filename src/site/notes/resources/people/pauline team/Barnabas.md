## Summary

Barnabas bridged the Jerusalem church and the Gentile mission, the original apostles and [[resources/people/pauline team/Paul\|Paul]], and failure and restoration in the case of [[resources/people/pauline team/John Mark\|John Mark]]. He saw potential in people others had written off, was a bridge for divided communities, and invested in the next generation at personal cost.

His birth name was Joseph. He was a Levite from Cyprus ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 4#4:36\|Acts 4#4:36]]), a priestly family raised in the diaspora. The apostles renamed him Barnabas, which Luke translates as “son of encouragement” (*huios paraklēseōs*). His first recorded act: selling a field and laying the proceeds at the apostles’ feet ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 4#4:37\|Acts 4#4:37]]).

Barnabas took Paul to the apostles after his conversion, vouched for him, and reported his bold preaching in Damascus ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 9#9:27\|Acts 9#9:27]]). The disciples were afraid of Paul. Without Barnabas’s credibility bridging that gap, Paul’s integration into the apostolic community might have stalled.

When Jerusalem heard that Gentiles in Antioch were coming to faith, they sent Barnabas to investigate ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 11#11:22\|Acts 11#11:22]]). He arrived, “saw the grace of God,” and “exhorted them all to remain faithful” ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 11#11:23\|Acts 11#11:23]]). Luke adds an editorial comment rare in Acts: “for he was a good man, full of the Holy Spirit and of faith” ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 11#11:24\|Acts 11#11:24]]). Barnabas recognized the scale of the Antioch work, traveled to Tarsus to recruit Paul ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 11#11:25-26\|Acts 11#11:25-26]]), and they taught together in Antioch for a full year. Bringing Paul into the Antioch work may have been the most consequential personnel decision in church history.

The Antioch church sent Barnabas and Paul out together as the first intentional equipping servant team ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 13#13:1-3\|Acts 13#13:1-3]]). On this journey through Cyprus and southern Asia Minor, Barnabas appears as the senior partner. At Lystra the locals identified Barnabas as Zeus and Paul as Hermes, suggesting Barnabas had the more commanding physical presence ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 14#14:12\|Acts 14#14:12]]). Luke calls both “apostles” ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 14#14:14\|Acts 14#14:14]]), one of the few times the title applies to anyone outside the Twelve.

The partnership fractured over two issues. At the Jerusalem Council, Barnabas and Paul stood together defending the Gentile mission ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 15#15:2, 12\|Acts 15#15:2, 12]]). At Antioch, when Peter withdrew from eating with Gentiles under pressure from the James party, Barnabas was “carried away” by the hypocrisy ([[resources/bible/epistles/Galatians 2#2:13\|Galatians 2#2:13]]). Paul named this as a betrayal of the gospel’s logic. Then, planning a second journey, Paul refused to bring [[resources/people/pauline team/John Mark\|John Mark]] because of his earlier desertion. Barnabas insisted (Mark was his cousin, [[resources/bible/epistles/Colossians 4#4:10\|Colossians 4#4:10]]) and the disagreement was so sharp (*paroxysmos*) that they separated ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 15#15:39\|Acts 15#15:39]]). Barnabas took Mark to Cyprus; Paul took [[resources/people/pauline team/Silas\|Silas]] to Syria and Cilicia.

Paul later acknowledged Mark’s usefulness ([[resources/bible/epistles/2 Timothy 4#4:11\|2 Timothy 4#4:11]]) and sent greetings through him ([[resources/bible/epistles/Colossians 4#4:10\|Colossians 4#4:10]]). If early church tradition is correct, Mark wrote the Gospel of Mark. Barnabas’s refusal to give up on a failed young worker may have produced one of the four canonical Gospels.

In [[resources/bible/epistles/1 Corinthians 9#9:6\|1 Corinthians 9#9:6]], Paul cites Barnabas as a fellow apostle who, like Paul, chose to work for his own living rather than depend on church support. The reference assumes the Corinthians knew and respected Barnabas, suggesting the relationship between the two men recovered at least in mutual respect.
