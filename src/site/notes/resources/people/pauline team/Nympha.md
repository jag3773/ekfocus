# Nympha

## Info
- #benefactor #female
- Home:: [[Laodicia\|Laodicea]]
- Other Locales:: Lycus Valley
- References:: [[resources/bible/epistles/Colossians 4#4:15\|Colossians 4#4:15]]

## Notes
- Hosted a house church in Laodicea
- One of several women in the Pauline network who provided material support and space for congregational gatherings

## Summary

Nympha appears once in the New Testament: [[resources/bible/epistles/Colossians 4#4:15\|Colossians 4#4:15]]. Paul writes: "Give my greetings to the brothers and sisters in Laodicea, and to Nympha and the church that is in her house."

Her gender has been a textual controversy. The Greek name in the accusative (*Nymphan*) could be feminine "Nympha" or masculine "Nymphas." Early manuscripts lacked accent marks, leaving it ambiguous. Some later scribes masculinized both the name and pronoun. The best early manuscripts (Vaticanus, the earliest corrector of Sinaiticus, several important minuscules) read the feminine pronoun *autēs* ("her house"). Modern critical editions and translations have restored the feminine reading. The masculine "Nymphas" has no known parallel in Greek; "Nympha" is well attested as a woman's name.

A church met in Nympha's house, which means she had the wealth and social standing to host a gathering of 20-40 people. The patron of a house church bore responsibility for hospitality, logistics, and often leadership. Other women filled this role across the Pauline network: [[resources/people/pauline team/Lydia\|Lydia]] in Philippi ([[resources/bible/gospels/Acts 16#16:40\|Acts 16#16:40]]), [[resources/people/pauline team/Priscilla\|Priscilla]] and [[resources/people/pauline team/Aquila\|Aquila]] in Rome and Ephesus ([[resources/bible/epistles/Romans 16#16:5\|Romans 16#16:5]], [[resources/bible/epistles/1 Corinthians 16#16:19\|1 Corinthians 16#16:19]]), and [[resources/people/pauline team/Apphia\|Apphia]] in Colossae ([[resources/bible/epistles/Philemon 1#1:2\|Philemon 1#1:2]]).

Nympha's house church was distinct from the Colossian congregation. Paul addresses the Laodiceans and Nympha separately and tells the Colossians to share their letter with the Laodicean church and vice versa ([[resources/bible/epistles/Colossians 4#4:16\|Colossians 4#4:16]]). The Lycus Valley Christians were organized as interconnected house churches across Colossae, Laodicea, and [[Hierapolis\|Hierapolis]], through the equipping servant [[resources/people/pauline team/Epaphras\|Epaphras]] ([[resources/bible/epistles/Colossians 4#4:12-13\|Colossians 4#4:12-13]]).

The early Christian movement had no dedicated meeting spaces. Benefactors who opened their homes, many of them women, provided key material and social infrastructure for the church to thrive.
